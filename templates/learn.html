What are exoplanets?

An exoplanet is just a planet that orbits a star outside our solar system.

Think of it like this: Earth, Mars, and Jupiter all orbit our Sun 'they’re regular planets'. But if there’s a planet orbiting another star far away in space, that’s an exoplanet (short for “extrasolar planet”).

They can be big like Jupiter, small like Earth, or totally strange types we don’t even have in our solar system. Some are scorching hot, some are frozen, and a few might even be in the right conditions for life!

What is the Transit Method?

The transit method finds planets by watching a star’s brightness. When a planet passes in front of its star, it blocks a little bit of the light, causing the star to look slightly dimmer for a short time. These dips in brightness show up as a repeating pattern called a light curve. see the video below to understand how it works.

  • Depth: how much the star’s light drops — linked to the planet’s size compared to the star.
  • Duration: how long the dimming lasts — depends on how fast the planet moves and the size of the star.
  • Period: how often the dips happen — tells us the length of the planet’s orbit (its year).

Why isn’t every dip a planet?

When we watch a star’s light, a dip usually means something passed in front of it. That “something” could be a planet… but not always! Sometimes we get tricked. Here are the 5 main reasons:

  1. Two stars, not one: If two stars orbit each other, one star can block the other, making a dip that looks like a planet’s shadow.
  2. Extra star nearby: A background star (in front of or behind) can sneak into the picture and cause dips too.
  3. Telescope mistakes: Small errors in the camera or sensors can create fake dips.
  4. A tricky star: Some stars get dimmer or brighter on their own because of spots, flares, or shaking.
  5. Space “static”: High-energy particles or glitches in the data can make false dips appear.

Bottom line: Not every dip means a planet. Scientists use lots of tests (and now machine learning) to figure out which ones are real.

What We Can Learn

  • Planet size: The bigger the dip, the bigger the planet (relative to the star).
  • Orbital period: The time between dips tells us how long the planet takes to go around the star.
  • Orbit path: The shape of the dip shows how the planet passes across the star.
  • Atmospheres: Looking at dips in different colors of light can reveal gases in the planet’s atmosphere.

Limitations

  • Alignment bias: Only systems with orbits nearly edge-on to us will transit.
  • False positives: Eclipsing binaries or stellar variability can mimic signals.
  • Small planets: Produce very shallow dips that require high precision and repeated observation.
